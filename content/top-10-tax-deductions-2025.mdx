## Don't Leave Money on the Table

Every year, millions of taxpayers miss out on valuable deductions simply because they don't know they exist. Whether you're a W-2 employee or self-employed, there are deductions available that could significantly reduce your tax liability.

## 1. Home Office Deduction

If you work from home, even part-time, you may qualify for the home office deduction. The IRS allows you to deduct a portion of your rent, mortgage interest, utilities, and insurance based on the percentage of your home used exclusively for business.

**Simplified method:** Deduct $5 per square foot of your home office, up to 300 square feet ($1,500 max).

## 2. Student Loan Interest

You can deduct up to $2,500 in student loan interest paid during the year, even if you don't itemize your deductions. This applies to both federal and private student loans.

## 3. Charitable Contributions

Beyond cash donations, you can deduct the fair market value of donated clothing, household items, and even mileage driven for charitable purposes (14 cents per mile in 2025).

## 4. Medical and Dental Expenses

If your unreimbursed medical expenses exceed 7.5% of your adjusted gross income, you can deduct the excess. This includes insurance premiums, co-pays, prescriptions, and even travel to medical appointments.

## 5. State and Local Taxes (SALT)

You can deduct up to $10,000 in state and local taxes, including property taxes and either state income tax or sales tax.

## 6. Self-Employment Tax Deduction

If you're self-employed, you can deduct the employer-equivalent portion of your self-employment tax (50% of the total self-employment tax) as an adjustment to income.

## 7. Health Insurance Premiums (Self-Employed)

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of their health insurance premiums for themselves, their spouse, and dependents.

## 8. Educator Expenses

Teachers and educators can deduct up to $300 for unreimbursed classroom supplies, professional development courses, and educational materials.

## 9. Energy-Efficient Home Improvements

The Residential Clean Energy Credit allows you to claim up to 30% of the cost of qualifying clean energy equipment, including solar panels, wind turbines, and geothermal heat pumps.

## 10. Retirement Contributions

Contributions to traditional IRAs may be tax-deductible, and contributing to a 401(k) reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. For 2025, you can contribute up to $23,500 to a 401(k) and $7,000 to an IRA.
