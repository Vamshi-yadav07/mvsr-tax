## Choosing the Right Business Structure

One of the most important decisions you'll make as a business owner is choosing the right legal structure. The two most popular options — LLC and S-Corporation — each offer distinct advantages depending on your situation.

## What Is an LLC?

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) is a business structure that combines the liability protection of a corporation with the tax flexibility of a partnership.

**Key Features:**

- Personal asset protection from business debts
- Pass-through taxation (profits taxed on your personal return)
- Flexible management structure
- Fewer compliance requirements than corporations

## What Is an S-Corp?

An S-Corporation is a tax election that can be made by an LLC or corporation. It allows the business to pass income directly to shareholders while potentially saving on self-employment taxes.

**Key Features:**

- Pass-through taxation
- Potential self-employment tax savings
- Must pay reasonable salary to owner-employees
- More strict compliance requirements

## Key Differences

| Feature | LLC | S-Corp |
|---------|-----|--------|
| Self-Employment Tax | All profits subject to SE tax | Only salary subject to SE tax |
| Salary Requirement | No | Yes, must pay reasonable salary |
| Ownership Limits | Unlimited members | Max 100 shareholders |
| Compliance | Minimal | Annual meetings, minutes, etc. |
| Best For | Small/new businesses | Profitable businesses saving on SE tax |

## When to Choose an LLC

- You're just starting out and want simplicity
- Your business profits are under $50,000-$60,000
- You want maximum flexibility in profit distribution
- You have a single-member business

## When to Elect S-Corp Status

- Your business profits exceed $60,000-$80,000
- You want to reduce self-employment taxes
- You can comply with additional reporting requirements
- You're willing to run payroll for yourself

## A Real-World Example

**Scenario:** Your business earns $120,000 in profit.

**As an LLC:** You'd pay approximately $17,000 in self-employment taxes on the full $120,000.

**As an S-Corp:** If you pay yourself a $60,000 salary, you'd pay approximately $9,180 in payroll taxes — saving nearly $8,000.
